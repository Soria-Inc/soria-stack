# mechanism-vs-event

The news peg is what happened. The mechanism is how the event produces
its effect. For most events the two are tightly coupled — "CMS finalizes
2.48% rate" has no separate mechanism worth surfacing. But for a subset
of events, the mechanism is non-obvious, the mechanism is the real
insight, and leading with the event buries the analyst's value-add.

## When to apply

Not every event. Use this skill when:

- The event has a measurable second-order effect that differs from the
  first-order implication (e.g. Medicaid work requirements reduce
  enrollment, but the mechanism is *administrative burden*, not job-
  availability).
- Prior evidence (analog jurisdictions, prior cycles, historical
  precedents) has already revealed the mechanism but the surface news
  doesn't name it.
- The mechanism is what makes the event contagious (why sibling states
  will follow, why other payers will copy, why similar rulings will
  extend).
- The mechanism, once named, changes the policy response or the
  investment thesis.

If none of these apply, skip this skill. Straight event framing is
fine for earnings prints, rate notices where the rate is the news,
simple M&A closes, and direct legal judgments.

## Core move

Write two sentences:

1. **The event.** What just happened. Subject + verb + specific facts.
   ("Indiana and Idaho adopted 3-month Medicaid work-rule lookbacks.")
2. **The mechanism.** How the event produces its intended or feared
   effect. ("In Arkansas's 2018 pilot, >80% of disenrollments came from
   failure to file paperwork, not from failure to meet the work
   threshold — administrative burden, not employment, is what drove
   coverage loss.")

Ask: which sentence would a PM want in the title? If the mechanism is
well-known or tightly coupled to the event, the event sentence wins.
If the mechanism is non-obvious or surprising, the mechanism sentence
wins — promote it to the title and demote the event to the `Driving
the news` section.

## Worked examples

**Example 1: Medicaid work requirements.**
- Event: "Indiana and Idaho adopt 3-month Medicaid work-rule lookbacks."
- Mechanism: "Arkansas's 2018 data shows paperwork, not employment,
  drives 80%+ of disenrollment."
- Verdict: mechanism is the insight. The PM wants to know why this
  matters, and the answer isn't in the lookback length — it's in the
  fact that the rule's stated purpose (verify work) diverges from its
  actual effect (cull enrollment via friction).
- Title should surface the mechanism. Options:
  - `Medicaid work rules cull enrollment through paperwork, not jobs`
  - `Red states pick strictest Medicaid work rules, blue states the
    loosest` (mechanism is implicit in the partisan axis)
- The event goes in `Driving the news` sentence 1.

**Example 2: 340B expansion.**
- Event: "Federal court upholds 340B drug-discount expansion to
  contract pharmacies."
- Mechanism: "Drug manufacturers can't refuse sales to hospitals
  routing 340B prescriptions through their contract-pharmacy
  networks, expanding the discount base by ~3x."
- Verdict: mechanism is the insight. "Contract pharmacy" is the key
  word — without it, the reader doesn't understand what changed.
- Title should name the contract-pharmacy mechanism.

**Example 3: CMS final rate notice.**
- Event: "CMS finalizes 2027 MA rate at 2.48%."
- Mechanism: None that's separate from the event. The rate IS the
  mechanism.
- Verdict: straight event framing. Title: `Trump administration raises
  2027 Medicare Advantage payments 2.48%, adding over $X to industry
  revenue` or similar. No mechanism-first framing needed.

**Example 4: RADV audit settlement.**
- Event: "UnitedHealth settles RADV audit with $X payment."
- Mechanism: "RADV extrapolation methodology — auditing 200 charts and
  extrapolating the error rate to the full population — is the source
  of the exposure. Settlement confirms the methodology holds."
- Verdict: mechanism is the insight for the *sector*, event is the
  insight for UNH. If the note is about UNH, event-first. If the note
  is about the MA sector, mechanism-first.

## The canonical output

A one-line mechanism statement that can either (a) replace the event
in the title, or (b) be promoted to the first sentence of `Why it
matters`. Examples:

- `paperwork, not employment, drives coverage loss`
- `RADV extrapolation multiplies single-chart errors by 1,000x`
- `contract-pharmacy expansion triples the 340B discount base`
- `the age-in default is what locks MA enrollment trajectory`
- `charity-care offsets, not commercial rates, drive dialysis margins`

If you can write a one-liner this tight, the mechanism is real and
belongs in the note's spine.

## Traps

- **Don't invent a mechanism to look analytical.** If the mechanism is
  just "companies lose revenue because prices dropped," that's not a
  mechanism worth surfacing. Save the mechanism framing for cases
  where the causal chain is non-obvious.
- **Don't over-promote the mechanism when the event is already famous.**
  For one-name earnings prints or high-profile M&A, readers know the
  mechanism; they want the number. Straight event framing wins.
- **Don't conflate mechanism with rationale.** Rationale is WHY the
  actor did the thing ("CMS wants to save money"). Mechanism is HOW
  the thing produces its effect ("the rate cut flows through to MLR
  because re-pricing happens January 1"). Analysts care about
  mechanism, not rationale.
- **Don't bury the event when you promote the mechanism.** The news
  peg still goes in `Driving the news` sentence 1 — reader must know
  what happened before they understand why the mechanism matters.
