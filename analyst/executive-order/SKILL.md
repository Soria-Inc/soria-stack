# executive-order

Presidential EOs move healthcare stocks more often because of framing
than economics. A headline like "Trump signs order to lower drug prices"
can move PBMs 4% in the session even when the operative text directs an
agency to "study" or "consider" rulemaking within 180 days. The value
of your note is distinguishing operative action from political signal,
and backing that up with the text.

## When to apply

- Presidential Executive Order (publication in Federal Register or WH
  archive).
- Presidential memorandum or proclamation with a healthcare directive.
- National Emergency declaration affecting provider/payer regulation
  (e.g., COVID-era 1135 waivers, controlled-substance prescribing).
- White House "Fact Sheet" that announces an EO or a forthcoming EO —
  treat as pre-announcement of the text.

## Core moves

1. **Three-bucket classification.** Every EO sorts into exactly one:
   - **Immediate self-executing.** The EO directs federal agencies to
     do something within their existing statutory authority, with a
     specific deadline, and the action has direct economic consequence
     (e.g., "HHS shall terminate X demonstration program within 60
     days"). These move the stock on the signing day for a reason.
   - **Requires rulemaking or legislation.** The EO directs an agency
     to initiate an NPRM, issue guidance, or ask Congress to act. The
     economic impact is 9–24 months out IF the rulemaking survives
     litigation and the next election. Price this heavily discounted.
   - **Pure political signal.** The EO reiterates existing policy,
     creates a taskforce, or directs a report. Zero direct economics.
     The stock reaction is pure narrative and will fade.

   Pick one bucket. If the EO has multiple provisions that sort
   differently, run the analysis per-provision.

2. **Time-phased ramp.** For buckets 1 and 2, the impact is not
   a step function — it's a schedule. Name the schedule. EO Section
   numbers frequently give dated commitments. A typical EO reads:
   "Within 60 days, the Secretary shall … Within 180 days, … Within
   one year, …". Translate this into a calendar of milestones and pair
   each milestone with the $/EPS impact that crystallizes IF that
   milestone lands on schedule.

3. **Legal survival probability.** Most market-moving healthcare EOs
   get sued. The 9th Circuit, DDC, and E.D. Tex. are the usual venues.
   Cite the relevant precedent — does the EO rest on a statute (likely
   survives) or on vague "inherent executive authority" (likely blocked
   at district court)? Name a probability. You are not a lawyer; you
   can still quote one.

4. **The lookback.** Find the last 2–3 comparable EOs (same admin or
   prior admin) that tried similar moves. What actually happened?
   "Trump's 2020 MFN EO was blocked in December 2020 by the N.D. Cal.,
   revoked by EO 14023 in January 2021" is the most useful sentence
   you can write about a new drug-pricing EO. History rhymes; the
   pattern is 60–90% of the signal.

5. **What the sector actually does on EO days.** The tape-vs-narrative
   move applies heavily here. If PBMs trade UP on an "anti-PBM" EO,
   the market read the text and classified it as bucket 3. Name the
   divergence, don't gloss.

## The canonical chart

Time-phased stacked column: X-axis = quarters from signing day, Y-axis
= $/EPS impact. Three stacks per quarter: (a) immediate provisions
(solid color), (b) rulemaking-contingent provisions (stripe or lighter
shade), (c) legislation-contingent provisions (lightest or dotted).
A horizontal line at signing-day's stock-move implied impact shows
what the market priced. Reader sees immediately whether the market
is pricing bucket 1 only or bucket 1+2.

Alternative for pure-signal EOs (bucket 3): don't chart $/EPS. Chart
the stock-move on signing day against the move on 2–3 comparable
prior EOs. The similar-size move IS the insight — this one will
probably fade too.

## Traps

- **Don't count rulemaking-contingent impact at 100% probability.**
  The EO directs; the rule is where the money lives. A 60% probability
  of rulemaking × 70% probability of surviving litigation = 42% of
  face-value impact. Say so.
- **Don't rely on press-release summaries.** The Fact Sheet is
  marketing; the signed text in the Federal Register is what matters.
  Pull the Federal Register citation and quote the operative verb
  ("shall terminate" vs. "shall consider"). The verb is the whole
  story.
- **Don't ignore the revocation clause.** Most EOs revoke or amend
  prior EOs in Section 1 or 2. Those revocations can be as material as
  the new provisions (e.g., revoking an Obama-era EO that created an
  OIG reporting requirement quietly dismantles a compliance regime).
- **Don't pretend the next election doesn't exist.** A bucket-2 EO
  with a 14-month rulemaking path that only the current admin supports
  has an embedded election-risk option. Name the date.
